# When to Take Social Security - Age 62, 67, or 70? Complete Guide 2026

Your Social Security claiming decision is one of the most important financial choices you'll make. The difference between claiming at 62 versus 70 can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars over your lifetime. This guide provides the analysis you need to make the optimal decision for your situation.

<li><strong>Delaying to 70:</strong> Increases benefits by 24% plus cost-of-living adjustments</li>

## Understanding Social Security Ages and Benefits

### Key Ages for Social Security

**Age 62 - Early Retirement:**
- Earliest claiming age
- Permanent reduction in benefits
- About 75% of full benefit amount

**Age 67 - Full Retirement Age (FRA):**
- 100% of calculated benefit (Primary Insurance Amount)
- No early retirement reduction
- Ability to earn unlimited income without benefit reduction

**Age 70 - Maximum Benefit:**
- Delayed retirement credits stop accruing
- 132% of full benefit amount (24% increase over FRA)
- Maximum possible monthly benefit

### How Much Social Security Will You Receive?

**2026 Average Benefits:**
- **Early retiree (62):** $1,298 monthly
- **Full retirement (67):** $1,848 monthly  
- **Delayed retirement (70):** $2,299 monthly

**Maximum Benefits for High Earners:**
- **At 62:** $2,710 monthly
- **At 67:** $3,822 monthly
- **At 70:** $4,873 monthly

Your actual benefit depends on your 35 highest-earning years and claiming age.

## The Financial Impact of Timing

### Lifetime Benefit Analysis

**Example: Worker with $2,000 FRA benefit**

**Scenario 1 - Claim at 62:**
- Monthly benefit: $1,500 (75% reduction)
- Annual benefit: $18,000
- Age 78 cumulative: $288,000
- Age 85 cumulative: $414,000

**Scenario 2 - Claim at 67:**
- Monthly benefit: $2,000 (100% of FRA)
- Annual benefit: $24,000  
- Age 78 cumulative: $264,000
- Age 85 cumulative: $432,000

**Scenario 3 - Delay to 70:**
- Monthly benefit: $2,480 (132% of FRA)
- Annual benefit: $29,760
- Age 78 cumulative: $238,080
- Age 85 cumulative: $446,400

### Break-Even Analysis

**Early vs. Full Retirement:**
- Break-even age: ~78
- If you live past 78, full retirement claiming wins
- If health concerns suggest shorter lifespan, early claiming may be optimal

**Full vs. Delayed Retirement:**
- Break-even age: ~80
- Delayed claiming wins for longevity
- Must weigh opportunity cost of delayed income

## When to Take Social Security at Age 62

### Good Reasons to Claim Early

**Health Concerns:**
- Serious health conditions affecting life expectancy
- Family history of shorter lifespans
- Current health issues requiring expensive treatment

**Immediate Financial Need:**
- Unemployment or reduced income capacity
- Spouse has died and household income dropped
- No other retirement savings available

**Investment Opportunity:**
- Can invest early benefits at higher returns
- Have other income sources to delay spending Social Security
- Sophisticated investment knowledge and risk tolerance

**Spousal Strategy:**
- Lower-earning spouse claims early while higher earner delays
- Maximizes household income over both lifespans
- Particularly effective with significant income differences

### Drawbacks of Early Claiming

**Permanent Reduction:**
- 25-30% reduction that never recovers
- Affects spouse's survivor benefits
- Reduces inflation adjustments on smaller base

**Earnings Test:**
- If you continue working, benefits may be reduced
- $1 reduction for every $2 earned over $22,320 (2026 limit)
- Benefits are recovered after FRA but creates complexity

## When to Wait Until Age 67 (Full Retirement Age)

### Advantages of FRA Claiming

**Full Benefits:**
- 100% of calculated Primary Insurance Amount
- No early retirement reduction
- Maximum spousal benefits available

**Flexibility:**
- No earnings test restrictions
- Can work and receive full benefits
- Optimal for many typical retirement scenarios

**Balanced Approach:**
- Reasonable middle ground
- Good for average life expectancy
- Allows for modest delays without major longevity risk

### Who Should Consider FRA Claiming

- Average health and life expectancy
- Need income at 67 for retirement lifestyle
- Want to balance longevity risk with income timing
- Spouse has already optimized their claiming strategy

## When to Delay Until Age 70

### Maximum Benefit Strategy

**Delayed Retirement Credits:**
- 8% increase per year from FRA to age 70
- Plus cost-of-living adjustments
- Compounds to 24% total increase

**Ideal Candidates for Delay:**
- Excellent health and longevity genes
- Comfortable living on other retirement income
- Higher-earning spouse in married couple
- Want to maximize survivor benefits for spouse

### Considerations for Delaying

**Opportunity Cost:**
- Missing 3 years of Social Security income
- Must have other income sources
- Market risk on alternative investments

**Longevity Risk:**
- Need to live past ~age 80 to maximize benefit
- Unknown health changes between 67-70
- Consider quality of life during claiming delay

## Spousal Social Security Strategies

### Basic Spousal Benefits

**Spousal Benefit Rules:**
- Up to 50% of spouse's FRA benefit
- Must be married at least 1 year
- Available at age 62 (reduced) or FRA (full)
- Spouse must have claimed their own benefit

**Divorced Spousal Benefits:**
- Marriage lasted 10+ years
- Currently unmarried
- Ex-spouse eligible for benefits (but doesn't need to claim)
- Can claim without affecting ex-spouse's benefits

### Advanced Spousal Strategies

**File and Suspend (No Longer Available):**
- Was eliminated in 2016
- Understanding helps avoid outdated advice

**Restricted Application (Limited Availability):**
- Only for those born before January 2, 1954
- Can claim spousal benefits while delaying own
- Rare strategy with specific requirements

**Optimal Claiming Strategies for Couples:**
1. **Higher earner delays to 70** (maximizes survivor benefit)
2. **Lower earner claims at FRA or 62** (provides household income)
3. **Consider total household longevity**

## Tax Implications of Social Security Income

### Federal Taxation of Benefits

**Income Thresholds (2026):**
- **Single filers:** Up to $25,000 - no tax; $25,000-$34,000 - 50% taxable; over $34,000 - 85% taxable
- **Married filing jointly:** Up to $32,000 - no tax; $32,000-$44,000 - 50% taxable; over $44,000 - 85% taxable

### Combined Income Calculation

**Combined Income Formula:**
Adjusted Gross Income + Nontaxable Interest + 50% of Social Security = Combined Income

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Roth IRA withdrawals don't count toward combined income
- Municipal bond interest counts (even though federally tax-free)
- Traditional IRA/401(k) withdrawals increase combined income

### State Taxation

**States That Tax Social Security:**
- Colorado, Connecticut, Kansas, Minnesota, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, New Mexico, Rhode Island, Utah, Vermont, West Virginia

**Utah-Specific Considerations:**
- Utah taxes Social Security benefits following federal rules
- Retirement tax credit available for qualifying seniors
- State income tax rate: 4.85% flat rate

## Coordinating Social Security with Other Retirement Income

### Withdrawal Strategies

**Tax-Efficient Sequencing:**
1. **Early retirement (62-67):** Taxable accounts and Roth withdrawals
2. **After claiming Social Security:** Balance traditional retirement accounts
3. **Age 73+:** Required minimum distributions from traditional accounts

**Managing Tax Brackets:**
- Use low-income years for Roth conversions
- Balance Social Security timing with other income
- Consider Medicare IRMAA thresholds

### Healthcare Considerations

**Medicare and Social Security:**
- Medicare enrollment at 65 regardless of Social Security claiming
- Part B premiums automatically deducted from Social Security
- IRMAA surcharges based on income from 2 years prior

## Common Social Security Mistakes

### 1. Claiming Too Early Without Analysis

**The mistake:** Automatically claiming at 62 without considering longevity
**The cost:** Hundreds of thousands in lifetime benefits
**The fix:** Perform break-even analysis for your situation

### 2. Not Coordinating Spousal Strategies  

**The mistake:** Both spouses claiming suboptimally
**The cost:** Significant household benefit reduction
**The fix:** Optimize as a couple, not individuals

### 3. Ignoring Tax Implications

**The mistake:** Not considering how Social Security affects overall tax strategy
**The cost:** Higher taxes on other retirement income
**The fix:** Integrate Social Security planning with tax planning

### 4. Misunderstanding Survivor Benefits

**The mistake:** Not optimizing for the surviving spouse
**The cost:** Lower survivor income for potentially decades
**The fix:** Consider both lifespans in claiming strategy

### 5. Working While Claiming Early

**The mistake:** Continuing to work while claiming before FRA
**The cost:** Temporary benefit reduction due to earnings test
**The fix:** Understand earnings test implications
